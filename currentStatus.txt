Looking at the codebase thoroughly, I can give you a comprehensive analysis of where EquiFlow stands and what's needed to make it a real, launchable product.

Let me visualize this clearly:Here's the honest, complete picture:

**What exists right now:** A beautifully crafted marketing website — animations, copy, pricing, FAQ, testimonials — all polished and working. The design language (Quiet Luxury, warm neutrals, GSAP animations) is genuinely strong and fits the positioning perfectly. The `config.ts` file is well-structured for content management.

**What doesn't exist yet:** The entire product. The database, the backend API, the authentication system, Plaid bank connections, the actual dashboard UI, billing, and the privacy enforcement layer — none of it is built. The "Start Free Trial" buttons go nowhere. The contact form doesn't save to anything.

---

**The five hardest engineering problems to solve:**

**1. Bank data aggregation** — Plaid covers US, UK, Canada well, but Europe requires PSD2/Open Banking, India requires the Account Aggregator framework, and Southeast Asia is largely fragmented with no single API. For a global product this is not one integration, it's five or six, each with separate regulatory approval processes.

**2. Privacy zones with actual security** — This isn't a UI toggle. If Partner A has "private" spending, that data must never touch Partner B's API session at the database row level. This requires row-level security (PostgreSQL RLS), field-level encryption, and a clear audit trail. Getting this wrong creates both a trust catastrophe and potential legal liability.

**3. The household pairing model** — Two adults, one shared financial view, different permission levels per account and per category. The data model is genuinely complex: accounts belong to individuals, categories can be shared or private, and the "shared view" must be computed dynamically without leaking private rows.

**4. Regulatory compliance by country** — GDPR demands the right to deletion and data portability. CCPA requires opt-out of sale. Financial data is categorized as sensitive personal data in most jurisdictions, meaning consent management, data residency, and breach notification requirements are all stricter than a typical SaaS product.

**5. The cultural UX problem for global reach** — "Household" finances are not universal. Multigenerational households in South Asia, communal savings in West Africa, strict personal account separation in Scandinavia — the product needs flexible "household models" rather than assuming the Western dual-income couple as the only persona.

---

**Recommended next step right now:** Wire the contact form to Resend or Mailchimp and deploy the landing page to collect waitlist emails. The marketing site is good enough to validate real demand before spending months on the backend. The hardest mistake in fintech is building the engine before confirming people will actually trust you with their bank credentials.
